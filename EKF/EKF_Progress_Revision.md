## 1. Where We Started — The Three Equations from the Advisor

The advisor gave us **three governing ODEs** that describe the physics of a
single zone. These are the mathematical foundation of the entire EKF.

### Equation 1 — Zone Temperature (Sensible Energy Balance)

$$
C_s \dot{T}_z =
- UA\,T_z - c_{pa}(m_{inf}+m_{sa})T_z
+ UA\,T_o + c_{pa}\,m_{inf}\,T_o + c_{pa}\,m_{sa}\,T_{sa}
+ Q_{bg} + f_c\,q^{occ}_{sens}\,N
$$

> The rate of change of zone temperature depends on heat lost through the
> envelope ($UA$), heat carried in by infiltration and supply air, background
> equipment heat, and occupant sensible heat.

### Equation 2 — Zone Humidity Ratio (Moisture Balance)

$$
M\,\dot{\omega}_z =
-(m_{inf}+m_{sa})\,\omega_z
+ m_{inf}\,\omega_o + m_{sa}\,\omega_{sa}
+ G_{bg} + g^{occ}_\omega\,N
$$

> Same structure as temperature but for moisture content of air.

### Equation 3 — Zone CO₂ Concentration

$$
M\,\dot{c}_z =
-(m_{inf}+m_{sa})\,c_z
+ m_{inf}\,c_o + m_{sa}\,c_{sa}
+ g^{occ}_{CO_2}\,N
$$

> CO₂ is diluted by fresh air and generated by occupants.

---

## 2. The Problem — Too Many Unknowns

Looking at the three equations, the advisor's unknowns that you **cannot
directly measure with a simple sensor** are:

| Unknown | Meaning |
| :--- | :--- |
| $UA$ | Building envelope heat transfer coefficient |
| $C_s$ | Thermal capacitance of the zone (how much heat the room stores) |
| $M$ | Dry air mass inside the zone |
| $m_{inf}$ | Infiltration air mass flow (air leaking through gaps) |
| $Q_{bg}$ | Background heat from equipment |
| $f_c \cdot q^{occ}_{sens} \cdot N$ | Occupant heat load |
| $G_{bg}$ | Background moisture source |
| $g^{occ}_\omega \cdot N$ | Occupant moisture load |
| $g^{occ}_{CO_2} \cdot N$ | Occupant CO₂ generation |

There are **9 unknowns** across three equations, and standard building sensors
only give us temperature, humidity, and CO₂. This is the classic "inverse
problem" — we need to *estimate* the physics from observed behaviour.

---

## 3. The Key Idea — Lump the Unknowns into Parameters

To make the problem tractable, we **grouped** the unknowns into a smaller set
of composite parameters. This is what the EKF actually estimates.

### Temperature Equation → 3 $\alpha$ parameters

$$
\alpha_o = \frac{UA + c_{pa}\,m_{inf}}{C_s}, \quad
\alpha_s = \frac{c_{pa}}{C_s}, \quad
\alpha_e = \frac{Q_{bg} + f_c\,q^{occ}_{sens}\,N}{C_s}
$$

The ODE becomes neatly:
$$
\dot{T}_z = -(\alpha_o + m_{sa}\,\alpha_s)\,T_z
+ \alpha_o\,T_o + m_{sa}\,\alpha_s\,T_{sa} + \alpha_e
$$

### Humidity Equation → 3 $\beta$ parameters

$$
\beta_o = \frac{m_{inf}}{M}, \quad
\beta_s = \frac{1}{M}, \quad
\beta_e = \frac{G_{bg} + g^{occ}_\omega\,N}{M}
$$

$$
\dot{\omega}_z = -(\beta_o + m_{sa}\,\beta_s)\,\omega_z
+ \beta_o\,\omega_o + m_{sa}\,\beta_s\,\omega_{sa} + \beta_e
$$

### CO₂ Equation → Reuses $\beta_o, \beta_s$ + 1 $\gamma$ parameter

$$
\gamma_e = \frac{g^{occ}_{CO_2}\,N}{M}
$$

$$
\dot{c}_z = -(\beta_o + m_{sa}\,\beta_s)\,c_z
+ \beta_o\,c_o + m_{sa}\,\beta_s\,c_{sa} + \gamma_e
$$

> **Why reuse $\beta_o$ and $\beta_s$?** Because physically the same air mass
> $M$ and infiltration flow $m_{inf}$ govern both moisture and CO₂ transport.
> The EKF exploits this coupling — it uses CO₂ and humidity data together to
> estimate the same two parameters, making the estimation more robust.

### Summary of the 7 Estimated Parameters

| Index | Symbol | What it physically means |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| 0 | $\alpha_o$ | Lumped heat loss + infiltration per unit capacitance |
| 1 | $\alpha_s$ | Airflow coupling to temperature (inversely relates to $C_s$) |
| 2 | $\alpha_e$ | Total internal heat gain per unit capacitance |
| 3 | $\beta_o$ | Infiltration rate per unit air mass |
| 4 | $\beta_s$ | Inverse of zone air mass ($1/M$) |
| 5 | $\beta_e$ | Moisture internal source per unit air mass |
| 6 | $\gamma_e$ | CO₂ generation per unit air mass |

And the **3 directly measured states**:

| Index | Symbol | Sensor |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| 7 | $T_z$ | Temperature sensor in zone |
| 8 | $\omega_z$ | Humidity sensor in zone |
| 9 | $c_z$ | CO₂ sensor in zone |

**This gives the full 10-state vector $X$ that was defined in the EKF_System_Reference.md.**

## 4. What We Actually Implemented for the Evaluation — The 8-State EKF

Here is the honest summary of what was done **quickly for the semester 7 evaluation**.

### What Was Dropped and Why

The Kaggle dataset we had (`Occupancy_Estimation.csv`) **did not contain
humidity data** ($\omega_z$). It only has:
- Temperature ($T_z$) — averaged from 4 sensors
- CO₂ ($c_z$) — from one sensor
- Occupancy count ($N$) — ground truth for validation

Because there was no $\omega_z$ to measure, we **removed the entire humidity
equation** from the implementation. This meant:

- $\beta_e$ (index 5 in the full 10-state system) was dropped
- $\omega_z$ (index 8) was dropped as a measured state
- $\omega_o, \omega_{sa}$ inputs were not needed

This reduced the system from **10 states to 8 states**:

```
Full design:   X = [α_o, α_s, α_e, β_o, β_s, β_e, γ_e, T_z, ω_z, c_z]
Evaluation:    X = [α_o, α_s, α_e, β_o, β_s,      γ_e, T_z,      c_z]
```

### What Else Was Simplified

In the full design, the EKF needs the following **real sensor inputs** at each
timestep:
- $T_o$ — outdoor temperature
- $T_{sa}$ — supply air temperature
- $C_{sa}$ — supply air CO₂
- $\omega_o$ — outdoor humidity
- $\omega_{sa}$ — supply air humidity
- $m_{sa}$ — supply air mass flow rate

Since we only had the Kaggle dataset (a real indoor dataset from a generic
room, **not from an AHU experiment**), none of these AHU inputs were available.
We substituted them with **pseudo-constants** (hardcoded typical values):

```python
T_SA  = 16.0     # supply air temperature  [°C]   ← fixed constant
C_SA  = 420.0    # supply air CO₂          [ppm]  ← fixed constant
C_O   = 420.0    # outdoor CO₂             [ppm]  ← fixed constant
M_SA  = 0.3      # supply air mass flow    [kg/s] ← fixed constant
```

Only $T_o$ (outdoor temperature) was taken from a real source — the EPW
weather file for Chicago, interpolated to match the Kaggle timestamps.

### The Measurement Vector Was Also Reduced

In the full 10-state design, the EKF corrects itself using **3 measurements**:
```
Z = [T_z_meas,  ω_z_meas,  c_z_meas]    ← full design (3×1)
```

In the evaluation implementation, since humidity was dropped:
```
Z = [T_z_meas,  c_z_meas]               ← evaluation (2×1)
```

So the measurement matrix $H$ is $2 \times 8$ instead of $3 \times 10$.

## 8. Do I Need to Derive the Three Equations Myself?

**Short answer: No. But you need to understand them deeply enough to defend them.**

The three equations (Temperature, Humidity, CO₂) are **not brand-new
derivations** — they are standard **first-principles thermodynamic energy
and mass balances** that are well established in the building physics literature.
They come from applying:

- **First Law of Thermodynamics** (energy balance) → Temperature ODE
- **Conservation of Mass** (for water vapour) → Humidity ODE
- **Conservation of Mass** (for CO₂ species) → CO₂ ODE

These types of RC-thermal-network models appear in dozens of published papers
(e.g., Gaikwad et al. in your own literature review, Radecki & Hencey, Zamani
et al.). Your advisor has formulated this specific version, likely tailored for
the AHU-driven single-zone case matching your experimental setup.

## 9. Is My Understanding of the Research Purpose Correct?

**Yes, you have got it exactly right.** Let me state it clearly so you can
articulate it confidently:

The three ODEs describe the physics of how a zone behaves. If you want to use
these equations **in a real controller** (e.g., MPC — Model Predictive Control),
the controller needs to *know the values* of $UA$, $C_s$, $M$, $m_{inf}$, etc.
at any given moment. But:

- You cannot "look up" $UA$ — it changes with window seals, wall ageing, season.
- You cannot "measure" $m_{inf}$ — there is no practical sensor for infiltration.
- You cannot know $N$ (occupancy) without a dedicated (expensive) sensor.

So **the purpose of the EKF is**: given only cheap sensors ($T_z$, $\omega_z$,
$c_z$) and the known inputs ($T_{sa}$, $m_{sa}$, $T_o$), continuously estimate
the hidden parameters so that a controller can use the physics equations
*accurately* in real time.

**The chain is:**

```
Cheap Sensors → EKF estimates hidden parameters → Controller uses physics equations → Smart HVAC decisions
```

Without the EKF, the controller would have to hard-code wrong values for $UA$,
$C_s$, etc., which breaks as soon as the real building differs from the textbook.
With the EKF, the system is **self-calibrating and adaptive**. That is the
research contribution.

## 11. How to Impress the Evaluation Panel on EKF Questions

The panel will likely probe you in one of three ways. Here is exactly what to say:

### Q: "What is the EKF doing in your project?"

> "I augment the physical state vector with unknown building parameters — $UA$,
> thermal capacitance, infiltration rate, and occupancy load — and treat them as
> slowly drifting states estimated recursively from cheap sensor data. The EKF
> linearises the non-linear building dynamics at each timestep using a Jacobian
> matrix, producing a continuously updated parameter estimate that a controller
> can use without ever measuring those quantities directly."

### Q: "Why EKF and not something simpler or more modern?"

> "A linear Kalman Filter cannot handle the multiplicative coupling in the
> governing equations — for example, $m_{sa} \cdot \alpha_s \cdot T_z$ is a
> product of an input, a parameter, and a state. That non-linearity requires
> either the EKF's first-order linearisation or the UKF's sigma-point
> approximation. I chose EKF for its lower computational cost, which is
> important for real-time embedded deployment. My literature review shows this
> is consistent with the consensus in the field — Radecki & Hencey (2012)
> and Zamani et al. (2024) both confirm EKF scales better for higher-dimensional
> building systems."

### Q: "How do you know your estimated parameters are correct?"

> "In the Kaggle dataset validation, the EKF-estimated occupancy $N_{est}$ is
> recovered from $\gamma_e / \beta_s$ and compared against the ground-truth
> occupancy count in the dataset — I do not use occupancy as an input at all.
> For the physical rig, we will use known heat sources (lightbulbs of known
> wattage) and controlled CO₂ injection, so the true values of $\alpha_e$ and
> $\gamma_e$ are calculable from first principles, providing a direct validation
> baseline."

### Q: "What is the limitation of your current implementation?"

> "The current implementation uses a Kaggle dataset from a generic room, not a
> controlled AHU experiment. The supply air inputs ($T_{sa}$, $m_{sa}$) were
> replaced with typical constants because the dataset did not include AHU
> measurements. The humidity equation was also omitted. These are acknowledged
> simplifications made for the proof-of-concept phase. The experimental rig will
> provide all the inputs needed for the full 10-state implementation with real
> sensor data."

### Q: "What is original about your work?"

> "Three things: first, the augmented-state formulation that lumps the
> building's unmeasurable parameters into a compact 7-parameter vector, allowing
> a single EKF to jointly estimate thermal, moisture, and CO₂ dynamics from
